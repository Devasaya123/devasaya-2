The Ajrakh Story

A living archaeology of textile

"Ajrakh is not merely a print on cloth — it is a 4,500-year-old prayer in indigo, madder and earth."

From the Indus Valley to Kutch

The story of Ajrakh begins long before history was written. Fragments of indigo-dyed cloth unearthed at Mohenjo-daro tell us that the people of the Indus Valley were already block-printing textiles five millennia ago. When the Khatri community migrated from Sindh into the desert state of Kutch in the 16th century, they carried this craft with them — and it has been kept alive, generation after generation, ever since.

Sixteen stages, one piece of cloth

An Ajrakh textile is the result of up to sixteen separate stages — washing, mordanting, resist-printing, dyeing with indigo and madder, sun-drying, river-rinsing, and pressing. Each stage is performed by hand. Each block is carved from teakwood by master craftsmen. Each colour comes from the earth — pomegranate rind, harde, lime, alum, iron, indigo, madder root.

Slow craft in a fast world

A single piece of Devasaya cloth can take three to four weeks to complete. The cloth is washed in the river, beaten on stone, dried under the desert sun. The motifs — eight-pointed stars, jaals, paisleys, mandalas — carry meaning passed down without words. When you wear Ajrakh, you wear a fragment of India's oldest textile memory.
